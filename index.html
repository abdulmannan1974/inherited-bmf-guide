Module 1: Foundations & phenotypes

Orientation

Why inherited BMF matters

  • Inherited syndromes account for roughly 20-30% of patients presenting with aplastic anaemia.
  • Phenotypes vary from single-lineage cytopenias to pan-lineage bone marrow failure.
  • Clinical anomalies are common, but up to one-third of FA patients have no obvious physical signs.
  • Accurate classification drives safer conditioning for HSCT and tailored surveillance for malignancy.

Module 2: Diagnostic framework & tests

Workup

Actionable diagnostic algorithm

1Identify AA/BMF or single-lineage cytopenia + family history or congenital features.
2Baseline labs: CBC with indices, reticulocytes, fetal Hb, erythrocyte ADA, marrow cellularity.
3Phenotype-driven screening: DEB/MMC breakage (FA), telomere length (DC), pancreatic function (SDS), marrow morphology (CDA), neutrophil studies (SCN).
4Targeted NGS panel (syndrome-specific) + constitutional karyotype.
5Confirm diagnosis, counsel family, and plan HSCT conditioning safely.

Module 3: Fanconi anaemia (FA)

Global BMF

Clinical features

  • Autosomal recessive disorder (X-linked rare) with progressive BMF.
  • Somatic anomalies: cafe-au-lait, radial ray defects, skeletal, GU, GI, cardiac, neuro anomalies.
  • Up to one-third have no obvious physical abnormalities.
  • Blood counts often normal at birth; pancytopenia develops insidiously, median age 7 years.
  • Cumulative BMF by age 40 is ~90%.
  • Median survival in IFAR cohort was 24 years (improving with modern care).

Malignancy risk

  • AML common, often FAB M4/M5.
  • Hematologic malignancy incidence ~33% by age 40.
  • Solid tumors ~28% by age 40 (hepatic tumors, SCC of vulva, esophagus, head/neck).
  • Median age: leukemia 13 years, solid tumors 25 years.

FA/BRCA pathway (Figma-style diagram)

Diagnostics & genetics

  • Chromosomal breakage after DEB/MMC remains a useful screening test.
  • 22 complementation groups; FA-A, -C, -G are most common.
  • FA-D1 (BRCA2) and FA-N (PALB2) carry high risk of childhood solid tumors.
  • Somatic mosaicism can lead to improved counts (natural gene therapy).

Module 4: Dyskeratosis congenita (DC)

Telomere disorder

Clinical core

  • Mucocutaneous triad: abnormal skin pigmentation, nail dystrophy, mucosal leukoplakia.
  • Multi-system involvement: dental, GI, GU, neuro, pulmonary, skeletal.
  • Skin/nail changes often by age 10; BMF typically before 20.
  • 80-90% develop BM abnormalities by age 30.

Diagnostic anchors

  • Minimal criteria: at least two major features (skin pigmentation, nail dystrophy, leukoplakia, BMF) plus two other features.
  • Main causes of death: BMF/immunodeficiency (~60-70%), pulmonary complications (~10-15%), malignancy (~10%).
  • X-linked, AD, and AR inheritance patterns.

Telomere biology (Figma-style diagram)

Genetic subtypes (Table 30.6)

DC genes cluster into telomerase and shelterin pathways. XLR DKC1 is classic; AD and AR forms involve TERT/TERC and multiple telomere regulators.

Show key DC genes by inheritance
X-linked: DKC1 (dyskerin).
Autosomal dominant: TINF2, TERC, TERT, NAF1, ZCCHC8, NPM1, ACD/TPP1, RTEL1, RPA1, MDM4, POT1.
Autosomal recessive: USB1, TYMS-ENOSF1, RTEL1, PARN, NOP10, TERT, NHP2, WRAP53/TCAB1, CTC1.
Treatment essentials
Oxymetholone responds in ~two-thirds (0.25 mg/kg/day up to 2-5 mg/kg/day). Danazol is increasingly first-line. HSCT is curative but high risk due to pulmonary/vascular complications; fludarabine-based non-myeloablative protocols show lower toxicity.

Module 5: Shwachman-Diamond syndrome (SDS)

Ribosomopathy

Clinical triad

  • Exocrine pancreatic insufficiency (100%) with early malabsorption and failure to thrive.
  • Bone marrow failure (100%), often neutropenia.
  • Skeletal abnormalities: metaphyseal dysostosis (~75%).

Key features

  • Short stature (~70%), protuberant abdomen, ichthyotic rash (~60%).
  • Neutropenia (~60%), pancytopenia (~20%).
  • MDS/AML risk ~25%, AML common (age 1-43 years).
  • Differential: Pearson syndrome (ringed sideroblasts, mitochondrial DNA defects).
  • Non-haematological malignancies have not been observed in SDS.

Ribosome biogenesis diagram

Genetics & management

  • SBDS variants in >90% of cases; DNAJC21, EFL1 (AR) and SRP54 (AD) cause SDS-like disease.
  • Pancreatic enzymes for malabsorption; G-CSF for neutropenia.
  • Oxymetholone can improve anemia/thrombocytopenia.
  • Leukemia often poor prognosis; HSCT with fludarabine conditioning preferred.

Module 6: Diamond-Blackfan anaemia (DBA)

Pure red cell aplasia

Diagnostic criteria

  • Macrocytic (or normocytic) anemia in early childhood.
  • Reticulocytopenia.
  • Normocellular marrow with erythroblasts <5%.
  • Normal or slightly low WBC; platelets normal or high.
  • Supportive: elevated eADA, macrocytosis, raised fetal Hb.

DBA Registry highlights

  • Incidence ~5 per million live births; median presentation 8 weeks.
  • 93% present in first year; 79% initially steroid responsive.
  • Actuarial survival >40 years: 100% sustained remission, 87% steroid-maintained, 57% transfusion-dependent.
  • Somatic anomalies in 47% (craniofacial, limb/hand, GU, cardiac).

Module 7: Congenital dyserythropoietic anaemia (CDA)

Dyserythropoiesis

CDA overview

  • Heterogeneous group with anemia and ineffective erythropoiesis.
  • Classical types I, II, III; additional types IV-VII described.
  • Characteristic marrow morphology and EM findings guide diagnosis.

CDA type I (CDAN1/CDIN1)

  • Mild-moderate anemia (Hb ~66-116 g/L) with macrocytosis in ~70%.
  • Marrow: megaloblastic precursors, internuclear chromatin bridges, 3-7% binuclearity.
  • EM: spongy ‚ÄúSwiss cheese‚Äù heterochromatin.

CDA type II (HEMPAS, SEC23B)

  • Most common subtype; anemia typically Hb 80-110 g/L.
  • Jaundice, hepatomegaly, splenomegaly; ~10% need regular transfusions.
  • BM: >10% binucleate erythroblasts; EM shows peripheral double membranes.
  • Acidified serum lysis (Ham test) usually positive.

CDA type III (KIF23)

  • Rare, autosomal dominant; giant multinucleated erythroblasts.
  • Increased prevalence of lymphoproliferative disorders reported.
  • KIF23 encodes a mitotic kinesin important in cytokinesis.

Module 8: Congenital & cyclic neutropenias

Neutropenia

Severe congenital neutropenia (SCN)

  • Neutrophils <0.2 x 10^9/L at birth; Hb and platelets usually normal.
  • Marrow maturation arrest at promyelocyte/myelocyte stage.
  • Severe infections; MDS/AML risk ~25% by age 25.
  • G-CSF dramatically improves outcome; HSCT if refractory or leukemia.

Cyclic neutropenia

  • Neutrophil nadir every ~21 days.
  • Fever and mouth ulcers around nadir.
  • Usually autosomal dominant; ELANE variants common.
  • ELANE mutations differ in location compared to SCN.

Module 9: TAR & CAMT thrombocytopenias

Thrombocytopenia

Thrombocytopenia with absent radii (TAR)

  • AR disorder: hypomegakaryocytic thrombocytopenia + bilateral radial aplasia.
  • Bleeding at birth; platelet count often <50 x 10^9/L.
  • Leukocytes normal or high (leukaemoid reaction).
  • Improves after infancy; prognosis excellent, no AA or leukemia reported.

Congenital amegakaryocytic thrombocytopenia (CAMT)

  • Isolated thrombocytopenia with absent megakaryocytes.
  • AR or X-linked; ~50% develop AA by age 5.
  • MPL variants in a subgroup; HSCT is treatment of choice for severe cases.

Module 10: New & overlapping BMF syndromes

Genomic era

Recently recognized BMF genes (Table 30.12)

  • AR: ERCC6L2, TPO/THPO, MYSM1, DUT, EXOC3L2, TP53.
  • AD: SAMD9, SAMD9L, SPI1.
  • Phenotypes overlap BMF, MDS, AML with extra-haematopoietic anomalies.

Familial MDS/AML genes

Common AD genes include RUNX1, CEBPA, TERC, TERT, GATA2, SRP72, ANKRD26, ACD/TPP1, ETV6, DDX41, RTEL1, PAX5, SAMD9, SAMD9L, MECOM, TP53, ERBB3, DHX34.

AR genes include MBD4, HLTF, XPC.

Overlap reminder
GATA2 and SAMD9/SAMD9L variants are common in pediatric MDS with monosomy 7 and can present as isolated cytopenias.

Module 11: High-yield consolidation

Exam-ready

Rapid-fire contrasts

  • FA: DNA repair defect + chromosomal breakage; DEB/MMC positive.
  • DC: telomere biology disorder; very short telomeres.
  • SDS/DBA: ribosome biogenesis defects (ribosomopathies).
  • SCN: promyelocyte arrest, G-CSF responsive, MDS/AML risk.
  • TAR: thrombocytopenia + absent radii; improves after infancy.

High-yield checklist

  • Look for congenital anomalies even in ‚Äúidiopathic‚Äù AA.
  • Choose HSCT conditioning based on syndrome (FA/DC are highly sensitive).
  • Use syndrome-targeted panels; avoid assuming single-gene disorders.
  • Monitor malignancy risks long-term (FA, SDS, DBA).
