Overview of Inherited BMF Syndromes

üéØ

Key Concept

Inherited BMF syndromes are constitutional/genetic disorders characterized by aplastic anaemia/bone marrow failure, usually associated with somatic abnormalities. They represent 20-30% of patients presenting with AA.

Clinical Presentation Patterns

  • May present at birth or at variable time thereafter
  • Can present in adulthood
  • May involve all lineages or single lineage initially
  • Single cytopenia may progress to pancytopenia
  • ~1/3 of patients have NO physical abnormalities

Why Recognition Matters

  • Impacts understanding of "idiopathic" AA pathophysiology
  • Guides appropriate HSCT conditioning protocols
  • Determines surveillance for malignancy
  • Enables genetic counselling for families
  • May present with AA/BMF as initial manifestation

Fanconi Anaemia (FA)

üìú

Historical Note

First described by Guido Fanconi in 1927. FA is an autosomal recessive disorder (X-linked in rare subset) with progressive BMF and increased predisposition to malignancy, especially AML.

Clinical Features

  • Progressive bone marrow failure
  • Increased malignancy risk (AML, solid tumours)
  • Somatic abnormalities (~70% of patients)
  • ~1/3 have NO physical abnormalities
  • Median survival: 24 years (improving with clinical care)

Natural History

  • Birth Blood count usually normal
  • 5-10 years Pancytopenia develops (median age 7)
  • ~40 years ~90% develop BMF
  • ~40 years 33% cumulative haematological malignancy
  • ~40 years 28% cumulative solid tumours

Treatment Principles

üéØ Curative: HSCT

  • Only curative treatment for BMF
  • Modified conditioning required - FA patients hypersensitive to cyclophosphamide and radiation
  • Low-dose cyclophosphamide (20-40 mg/kg)
  • 4.5-6 Gy thoracoabdominal irradiation
  • HLA-identical sibling: ~70% 2-year survival
  • Unrelated donor: 20-40% survival

üìã Supportive Care

  • Androgens (oxymetholone, danazol) - 50-70% response
  • Blood product support
  • Surveillance for malignancy
  • Gene therapy trials (FA-A patients)

Critical Warning: Post-HSCT Malignancy

FA patients who undergo HSCT have a higher incidence of non-haematological malignancies (particularly head and neck SCC), usually 8-10 years post-transplant. Lifelong surveillance is essential.

Exam Pearl: Hoyeraal-Hreidarsson Syndrome

HH syndrome is a severe variant of DC characterized by intrauterine growth restriction, microcephaly, cerebellar hypoplasia, AA, and immunodeficiency. Often caused by DKC1 variants but also other genes.

Important: TERC/TERT Variants Beyond DC

Heterozygous TERC and TERT variants are risk factors for some cases of "idiopathic" AA and have been identified in patients with MDS. This highlights the overlap between inherited and acquired BMF.

Shwachman-Diamond Syndrome (SDS)

üîë

Key Features

SDS is characterized by exocrine pancreatic insufficiency (100%), BMF (100%), and skeletal abnormalities. It's a disorder of ribosome biogenesis.

Clinical Features

  • Pancreatic insufficiency - malabsorption, failure to thrive (may improve with age)
  • BMF - neutropenia most common (~60%), other cytopenias (~20% pancytopenia)
  • Short stature (~70%)
  • Skeletal abnormalities
  • Metaphyseal dysostosis on X-ray (~60%)
  • Hepatomegaly, rib/thoracic cage abnormalities

Haematological Abnormalities

  • Neutropenia - ~60% (intermittent or persistent)
  • Pancytopenia - ~20%
  • MDS/AML - ~25% by 40 years
  • Often with complex karyotypes
  • Poor response to conventional chemotherapy
  • Only HSCT is curative for leukaemia

Molecular Biology

The SBDS gene on 7q11.21 encodes a protein important in the maturation of the 60S ribosomal subunit. SDS is principally a disorder of ribosome biogenesis, linking it mechanistically to DBA.

Differential Diagnosis: SDS vs Pearson Syndrome

Shwachman-Diamond

  • Neutropenia > anaemia
  • No ringed sideroblasts
  • Normal mitochondrial DNA
  • Metaphyseal dysostosis
  • Better prognosis

Pearson Syndrome

  • Anaemia > neutropenia
  • Ringed sideroblasts present
  • Mitochondrial DNA deletions
  • Liver dysfunction, acidosis
  • Worse prognosis (death by age 5)

Diamond-Blackfan Anaemia (DBA)

üìú

Historical Note

Red cell aplasia was first reported by Josephs in 1936. Diamond reported on four children with hypoplastic anaemia in 1938. This is now recognized as DBA or congenital pure red cell aplasia.

Diagnostic Criteria for DBA

  1. Normochromic, usually macrocytic anaemia (occasionally normocytic)
  2. Developing in early childhood
  3. Reticulocytopenia
  4. Normocellular BM with selective deficiency of erythroid precursors (<5%)
  5. Normal or slightly decreased leukocyte counts

Clinical Features

  • Presents in early infancy (median 8 weeks)
  • 93% present in first year of life
  • Pallor, failure to thrive
  • 47% have somatic abnormalities
  • Craniofacial (50%), thumb malformations (38%)
  • Cardiac (30%), genitourinary (39%)

DBA Registry Data (DBAR)

  • Elevated erythropoietin
  • Family history (8.8% have >1 affected member)

Exam Pearl: HEMPAS

CDA Type II is also called HEMPAS (Hereditary Erythroblastic Multinuclearity with Positive Acidified Serum test). The positive Ham test (acidified serum lysis) distinguishes it from PNH - in CDA II, red cells are lysed by some acidified sera but NOT by the patient's own serum.

Congenital and Cyclical Neutropenias

üîë

Overview

Congenital neutropenia is a heterogeneous disorder including Kostmann syndrome (first described 1954). The neutropenia is usually recognized at birth with ANC often below 0.2 √ó 10‚Åπ/L. Associated with severe infections and early death without treatment.

Severe Congenital Neutropenia (SCN)

  • ANC usually <0.2 √ó 10‚Åπ/L
  • Hb and platelets usually normal
  • BM: Maturation arrest at promyelocyte/myelocyte stage
  • Abundant promyelocytes, reduced neutrophils
  • Severe infections without treatment
  • ~25% develop myeloid leukaemia by 25 years
  • No patients have developed AA

Cyclical Neutropenia

  • Neutrophil count cycles with ~21-day periodicity
  • ANC reaches nadir (may be zero)
  • Fever and mouth ulcers at nadir
  • Usually AD inheritance
  • Also caused by ELANE mutations
  • Variants cluster at different site than SCN

Molecular Insight: ELANE Mutations

ELANE encodes neutrophil elastase, a serine protease synthesized predominantly at the promyelocytic stage. Variants lead to accumulation of non-functional protein which triggers the unfolded protein response (UPR) leading to maturational arrest and apoptosis.

Congenital Thrombocytopenias: CAMT & TAR

CAMT (Congenital Amegakaryocytic Thrombocytopenia)

Presentation: Infancy with isolated thrombocytopenia, reduction/absence of megakaryocytes in BM, no somatic abnormalities

Evolution: ~50% develop AA usually by age 5 years

Genetics: Biallelic variants in MPL (thrombopoietin receptor) - 1p34

Lab: Elevated TPO levels, normal leucocyte and Hb

Treatment: HSCT if compatible donor; platelet transfusions for severe bleeding

TAR (Thrombocytopenia with Absent Radii)

Presentation: Hypomegakaryocytic thrombocytopenia + bilateral radial aplasia at birth

Key Feature: Thumbs are present (distinguishes from FA)

Other features: Absent ulnae, absent humeri, clinodactyly, microcephaly

Genetics: Compound heterozygosity - deletion + regulatory SNP in RBM8A

Prognosis: Platelet count usually improves after first year; good prognosis with supportive care

Exam Pearl: TAR vs Fanconi Anaemia

Both TAR and FA can present with radial abnormalities. The key distinguishing feature: in TAR, thumbs are PRESENT despite absent radii. In FA, thumbs may be absent or hypoplastic. Also, TAR patients have normal chromosome breakage studies.

  • TAR: RBM8A; thumbs present
  • Distinguish TAR from FA
  • HSCT for severe CAMT
  • TAR often improves with age

Conclusion

Since identification of the first FA gene (FANCC) in 1992, there have been significant advances in understanding FA, DC, and other BMF syndromes. This has facilitated diagnosis and highlighted the importance of studying rare syndromes for insights into common diseases. The connection between:

  • DC and AA - via defective telomere maintenance
  • DBA, SDS, and MDS - via defective ribosome biogenesis

These advances suggest that new treatment strategies targeting primary defects in each syndrome are likely to emerge.
