Module 1: What changed in ELN 2025

Orientation

Core guidance

  • WHO 2022 reframes CML as a biphasic disease and lowers BP threshold to >=20% blasts.
  • Milestones now framed as favorable, warning, unfavorable (vs optimal/warning/failure).
  • More emphasis on dose reduction for toxicity and individualized switching decisions.
  • TFR data matured, with clearer entry criteria and monitoring schedules.

Clinical nuance

ELN could not fully endorse either WHO or legacy AP definitions, but recognizes high-risk indicators in CP. Evidence since 2020 drives a more patient-specific approach to switching, particularly in older patients where deep molecular response may not justify higher-toxicity TKI.

Module 2: Diagnosis and baseline testing

Confirm CML

Baseline essentials

  • Diagnosis requires detection of BCR::ABL1 (Ph+ t(9;22)).
  • Marrow morphology + CBA, RT-qPCR on PB or BM.
  • Identify transcript type to enable precise monitoring.

Recommended baseline workup (ELN)

Module 3: Classification and risk stratification

Risk framing

Core guidance

  • ELTS replaces Sokal/Euro/EUTOS for predicting CML-related mortality.
  • WHO 2022: BP if blasts >=20%; AP category removed, high-risk indicators emphasized.
  • High-risk ACA are warning signs and influence monitoring and therapy selection.

High-risk indicators (WHO 2022)

  • Blasts 10â€“19%, PB basophils >=20%.
  • High ELTS score.
  • ACA: 3q26.2 rearrangements, -7, i(17q), complex karyotype.
  • Clusters of small megakaryocytes with fibrosis.

Module 4: Monitoring and response definitions

Molecular tracking

Monitoring cadence

  • Blood counts every 2 weeks until CHR.
  • RT-qPCR at least every 3 months until MMR confirmed.
  • After stable MMR: every 4â€“6 months.
  • More frequent monitoring if levels rise or fluctuate.

Atypical transcripts: use personalized RT-qPCR or RT-ddPCR; CBA is insufficient for routine monitoring but is useful when RT-qPCR cannot quantify or when resistance/progression is suspected. FISH can help in rare transcript follow-up.

Module 5: Milestones and response categories

Decision points

ELN 2025 categories

  • Favorable: switch unnecessary.
  • Warning: consider switch if trend or context supports.
  • Unfavorable: switch preferred.
  • Always interpret in context of trends and adherence.

Module 6: First-line therapy selection

Choose the TKI

Core guidance

  • First-line options: imatinib, dasatinib, nilotinib, bosutinib, asciminib (some countries), radotinib (Korea).
  • 2GTKI yield faster/deeper responses vs imatinib, without OS benefit.
  • Goal (OS vs TFR) and comorbidities should guide choice.

Comorbidity-driven selection

  • Pulmonary disease: avoid dasatinib (pleural effusion/PAH risk).
  • GI/renal/hepatic issues: consider avoiding bosutinib.
  • Diabetes or high cardiovascular risk: avoid nilotinib.
  • High ELTS score: consider more potent TKI upfront.

Asciminib (ASC4FIRST): higher MMR at weeks 48/96 vs investigator choice; fewer grade â‰¥3 AEs and fewer discontinuations; FDA approval for first-line in 2024.

Module 7: Resistance and mutation-guided therapy

Precision switch

When to test for mutations

  • Resistance or warning response.
  • Progression to or presentation in BP.
  • Relapse after alloSCT if prior mutation known.
  • Not recommended after loss of MMR post-TFR.

Always assess adherence, drug interactions, and TKI exposure before labeling resistance.

Sequencing

Second-line guidance

  • 30â€“40% switch from first-line due to intolerance or resistance.
  • Dose reduction is appropriate if response is good and toxicity is manageable.
  • Serious AEs require switching rather than dose reduction.

Dose reduction ranges + serious AEs

  • Imatinib: 400 â†’ 100â€“300 mg daily.
  • Dasatinib: 100 â†’ 20â€“50 mg daily.

  • Switch for recurrent pleural effusion, PAH, VOE/AOE, enterocolitis, serious neurologic issues, immune-mediated myocarditis/hepatitis/nephritis.

Beyond second-line

  • If resistant to 2GTKI: consider early ponatinib or asciminib.
  • ASCEMBL: asciminib superior to bosutinib after â‰¥2 TKIs.
  • OPTIC: dose-optimized ponatinib with reduced AOE risk.

Third-line context

OPTIC: 45/30/15 mg ponatinib with mandatory reduction to 15 mg once BCR::ABL1IS < 1%. Higher CCyR rates at higher starting dose; AOE rates lower than PACE. Asciminib phase I shows durable MMR with manageable AEs. Olverembatinib (China) active in T315I and multi-TKI resistant CP. Begin donor search at resistance to 2GTKI; refer for transplant at resistance to ponatinib or asciminib.

Module 9: Advanced phase and blast crisis

High urgency

Core guidance

  • Advanced phase is rare in high-income settings but common in delayed diagnosis.
  • Flow cytometry to define BP phenotype; check CNS involvement for lymphoblastic BP.
  • BP treatment: intensive chemo + TKI (dasatinib or ponatinib) â†’ alloSCT if eligible.

Clinical nuance

Presentation in advanced phase may do better than progression on TKI. Non-eligible patients may receive low-intensity chemo + TKI (clinical trial options emerging).

Module 10: Allogeneic stem cell transplantation

Curative option

When to consider alloSCT

  • CP resistant to 3rd/4th generation TKI or intolerant to all available TKI.
  • Presentation in or progression to BP.
  • Begin donor search at resistance to first 2GTKI.

Key points

  • Outcomes improved with reduced-intensity conditioning and haploidentical donors.
  • Best results in CP1; advanced phase outcomes poorer.
  • Post-alloSCT: q3 monthly molecular monitoring early, then q6 monthly; lifelong follow-up.

Stopping safely

Module 12: Parenting, pregnancy, adverse events

Special situations

Parenting guidance

  • Men: no consistent evidence of harm; continue TKI during conception attempts.
  • Women: avoid TKI during pregnancy; IFN is safe; imatinib/nilotinib may be considered after week 16.
  • Breastfeeding: avoid TKI; tailor duration to disease control.

Adverse events focus

Long-term TKI toxicity management is central: baseline co-morbidity assessment, ongoing surveillance, and proactive adjustment. Notable AE patterns include vascular events (nilotinib/ponatinib), pleural effusion (dasatinib), diarrhea (bosutinib), and metabolic effects.
